## 2. The big disagreement: long-press thumb-walk is the wrong PRIMARY selector for a voice-first operator

The touch brief (BD-B) makes the long-press + thumb-up/down level-walk the hero interaction. It's clever and I'd keep it — **but it is a two-hand-feeling, fine-motor, sustained-pressure gesture**, and it's the *most* fragile part of the whole design on a phone (it fights scroll, fights native selection, needs haptic calibration, needs real-device tuning he can only verify himself). For a one-handed thumb operator it's also reach-limited: the top third of a large iPhone screen is unreachable by the holding thumb without a grip shift.

**Voice dissolves the hardest half of that gesture.** The thumb-walk exists to answer one question: *"which scale — word / sentence / paragraph / whole section?"* That is a question you can simply **say**:

- Long-press to *anchor* a point (keep this — pointing is genuinely spatial). Then instead of holding and sliding the thumb up through levels, **speak the level**: *"the whole paragraph," "this bullet," "everything under this heading," "just that sentence," "the whole list."*
- The document tree (BD-C, which has to exist anyway) makes those words resolvable: anchor point → deepest node → the spoken level-word selects an ancestor by *type*, not by counting thumb-ticks.

This is strictly easier one-handed: a *tap* to anchor (any reach handled by scrolling the target to the thumb zone first) + a *spoken scale*. No sustained press, no precise vertical drag, no haptic-tick counting, no fighting scroll. **The thumb-walk becomes the eyes-free / noisy-room fallback for when he can't or won't speak.** Keep it; demote it.

This directly honours his own later note (MSG2): *"I actually kind of like a lot of the current selection, so maybe the long hold is for when I want to do other actions / be more granular."* He's already telling you the long-hold is the *advanced/secondary* path. Voice is the everyday path.
