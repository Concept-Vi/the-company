## 5. The overlay (BD-G) reframed as a voice-command surface

He wants the V to open a radial command center. Good — but for a voice-first operator the V has a second job: **it's the push-to-talk button and the "what can I say" affordance.**

- **V as PTT:** press-and-hold the V = talk; release = done. This solves the single biggest VUI fear (accidental activation / always-listening) and the privacy concern of an always-on mic, *and* it's perfectly thumb-reachable because he asked for the V to sit in the thumb zone and "open toward the thumb." One control, two roles: tap to open the menu, hold to talk.
- **Discoverability — "what can I say?"** The chronic VUI failure (the ResearchGate "What can I say?" paper names it exactly) is users not knowing the vocabulary. Because he is *not* a developer and *cannot* be told a command list to memorize, the system must **show the current valid voice commands contextually** — when a target is selected, the overlay shows "say: whole paragraph · this sentence · this list · send · attach". The available grammar is always on screen, so he never guesses. This also makes the voice path self-teaching.
- **Radial = speakable too:** each radial slice has a spoken label, so "switch to element mode" works without a tap. Mode-switch by voice is important because **in UI/element mode his hand may be busy pointing at the element** — saying "element mode" frees the gesture.
