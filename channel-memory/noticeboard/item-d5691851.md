## 5. Thumb-zone reality: the V and the gesture compete for the same real estate

This is the cross-cutting ergonomic fact that ties the whole feature together and that nobody reasoning about envelopes will catch:

On a modern iPhone held one-handed, the **comfortable thumb arc covers roughly the bottom third and the near-side edge.** The top corners are *unreachable* without a grip shuffle (the well-known "thumb-zone" / reachability maps). This has sharp consequences for Tim's specific asks:

- **MSG2: "hamburger in the **top-left**, document switcher on the title."** Top-left is the *worst* thumb target on the phone — the literal hardest pixel to reach one-handed (for a right-handed grip). His own "save to home screen, one-handed, non-obtrusive" goals fight his "hamburger top-left" instinct. I'd flag this honestly: **either move document-switching into the V/bottom zone, or accept that the top bar is a "two-handed / deliberate" action** (which is fine for *switching documents* — a rare, deliberate act — but would be wrong for anything frequent). The principle: *frequency determines altitude.* Frequent → bottom/thumb. Rare/deliberate → top is acceptable. Document-switch is rare, so top-left is *tolerable* there specifically — but the *comment/submit/mode* actions must never live up top.
- **The V must sit in the bottom thumb-arc, on the side he holds** — ideally **side-aware** (or a settings flip), because a bottom-left V is brutal for a right-handed grip and vice-versa. "The V opens toward the thumb" (BD-G) is right; make the V *position* configurable too, not just its bloom direction.
- **The level-scrubber and the V both want the bottom-third.** When he long-presses content low on the screen, his level-chip and the V button can collide. The chip should always render *opposite* the contact point (press low → chip floats above the thumb; press high → below), and the V should dim/yield while a gesture is armed.
