## 1. The core gesture is good instinct — but it collides head-on with iOS itself

Tim's mechanism — *long-press to arm, then slide thumb up/down to change the selection scale, release to commit* — is a **real, named family of interactions**, and his instinct is sound. It's a *press-and-drag-to-modulate-a-continuous-parameter* gesture. The closest shipping cousins:

- **iOS keyboard space-bar trackpad** (long-press the spacebar → the keys blank out → slide to move the cursor). This is the single most important precedent because *Tim already does it every day.* It proves the exact primitive he's describing — hold to enter a mode, move to scrub a value, lift to commit — already lives in his muscle memory.
- **The text-selection loupe / magnifier** (touch-and-hold on text → magnified cursor you drag).
- **Apple Pencil Pro squeeze** (WWDC24) → squeeze pops a contextual tool palette without lifting; haptic confirms. Same "modify without releasing" philosophy.

So the pattern is legitimate. **But here is the hard, specific problem nobody downstream will flag:** the gesture lives on top of mobile Safari, and Safari has *already claimed* long-press and vertical-drag.

- **Long-press on text in Safari = native text selection + the callout bar** (Copy / Look Up / Share). This fires at ~**0.5s**.
- **Long-press on an element with any link/image = the native preview/share sheet.**
- **Vertical drag = scroll.** This is the deepest reflex on the entire platform.

Tim's gesture wants long-press to mean "arm my selector" and wants vertical thumb-drag to mean "change scale." **Both are already taken.** If you don't actively suppress the natives, the user long-presses a paragraph and gets Apple's blue selection handles and a Copy/Paste bar instead of his level-picker — and when he drags up to go coarser, *the page scrolls away under his thumb.* That is not a polish bug; it's a total failure of the core interaction. The MacRumors and Reddit threads I found are full of exactly this rage about iOS selection mis-firing at the sentence/paragraph boundary with "no haptics at all" — which is *the precise granularity Tim is targeting.* He is walking straight into the most-complained-about interaction on the platform.

**What this forces (concrete):**
- The annotatable content must be rendered with `-webkit-user-select: none` and `-webkit-touch-callout: none` in annotation mode, so the native selection/callout never appears. The selector *replaces* native selection rather than competing with it.
- The arming long-press needs a **custom timer (~350–400ms is the sweet spot — shorter than Apple's 500ms callout so yours wins the race, but long enough not to fire on a scroll-flick)**, and on the very first move you must call `preventDefault()` / `touch-action: none` on the gesture surface to kill scroll *for the duration of the hold only*.
- Critically: **scroll must still work normally when the gesture is NOT armed.** The page is a document he reads by scrolling. So the gesture surface can't globally disable `touch-action` — it disables it *only* in the armed window, then restores. This is the single trickiest engineering moment in the whole feature and it's invisible until you feel it.

His own MSG2 instinct — *"maybe the long hold is for when I want to do **other** actions… be more granular than the current selection"* — is actually the escape hatch from this collision, and it's the right one. See §3.
