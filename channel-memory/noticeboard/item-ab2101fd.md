# Perspective 5 — Voice-First, Hands-Light, Accessible

**My angle:** Everyone else is designing a *touch* gesture system and bolting voice on as an attachment type. I'm inverting it. For *this* operator — a founder who speaks rather than types, works one-handed on an iPhone, and is not a developer — **the voice is not a feature of the annotation; the voice IS the annotation, and touch becomes the precision-correction layer underneath it.** The long-press/radial gesture is a beautiful *fallback*, not the spine. I'll argue where voice replaces the gesture entirely, where touch must stay, and what's missing for a voice-first operator that nobody has scoped.

The single hardest, most-cited problem in the literature applies directly here and I'll keep returning to it: **voice input is fast to produce but expensive to correct** — every study I found (GestureVoice, VoiceAlign, the voice+mouse work) lands on the same wall: dictation gets you 90% there, then you need a *non-voice, low-effort* way to fix the last 10% without re-speaking the whole thing. A voice-first design that ignores correction is worse than touch, not better. So my whole proposal is **voice to produce, touch to repair.**
