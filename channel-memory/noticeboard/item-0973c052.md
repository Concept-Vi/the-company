## 2. "Thumb up = coarser, down = finer" is fighting the body. Reconsider the axis.

Tim reasons: long-hold a word, **slide up** → sentence → paragraph → section (coarser); **slide down** → finer. It reads clean on paper. Through the ergonomics lens it has two real problems:

**Problem A — vertical is the most overloaded, least precise thumb axis.** On a phone held one-handed, the thumb pivots from the base joint; its *natural arc is a diagonal sweep*, and pure-vertical travel is the motion most easily confused with scroll. Vertical is also where the document content lives — so as he scrubs up to "coarser," the thing he's selecting is scrolling *the opposite way* in his peripheral vision. Vertical drag for a discrete level-picker is asking the hardest axis to do delicate work.

**Problem B — it's an unbounded-drag for a *discrete, small* set of levels (typically 3–6: word/sentence/paragraph/list/section/page).** Continuous drag is the right control for a *continuous* value (cursor position, volume). For 3–6 discrete stops it's overkill and feels mushy — how far is "one level"? 40px? 60px? Miss the threshold and you commit the wrong scale. This is the classic *gesture-for-discrete-choices* anti-pattern.

**What I'd propose instead (still his gesture, retuned):**

- **Keep "press-and-slide-to-pick-level," but make it a stepped picker with hard detents, not a free scrub.** Each level is a *zone* with a snap. Crossing into the next zone fires a **haptic tick** (`navigator.vibrate` on Android; on iOS Safari, the only reliable lever is the `<input switch>`/`<label>` haptic hack or a short audio-click fallback — be honest that **iOS Safari has no real Taptic API for web**, so "haptic on every level change" is a *needs-Tim, device-verified, probably-degraded* item, never assumed-green). The tick is what makes it feel mechanical and certain instead of analog and vague.
- **Show a live, persistent floating "level chip" pinned just above the thumb** ("Sentence" → "Paragraph" → "Whole section"), with a **translucent outline drawn around exactly what will commit** *right now*. Commit-on-release then feels like releasing a drawer that's already shown you its contents. This directly answers his "maybe it highlights what it's selecting and when I release that selects the level."
- **Reconsider the axis as a small fan/arc rather than a vertical line.** Because the thumb's comfortable motion is a diagonal arc, the levels could fan out as 3–5 stops along that arc (this is literally a *miniature radial/marking-menu* — see §4) so each level sits where a thumb naturally rests, not stacked vertically into the scroll axis. This also frees pure-vertical for scroll-passthrough if he overshoots the gesture.

I'd push back on Tim here, gently and explicitly, because this is exactly where his (honestly-stated) lack of design vocabulary meets a real ergonomic constraint he can't have known: **the up/down idea is the right *concept* (modulate scale without lifting) attached to the wrong *axis and control-type* (vertical free-drag).** The fix preserves everything he wants and feels better.
