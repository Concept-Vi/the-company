## 3. Comment vs. Suggestion vs. Change-request — the intent split the brief gestures at but doesn't name

Tim wrote: *"sometimes I might be making comments that I want a specific thing to be updated, other times it will be on something else."* That sentence is the **single most important product distinction in this entire category**, and the mature tools have a hard, visible split for it:

- **Google Docs:** *Comment* (a thread) vs. **Suggesting mode** (a concrete proposed edit you Accept/Reject). The accept-button is the magic — the suggestion *is* the diff.
- **GitHub:** a review *comment* vs. a **suggested change block** (```suggestion) that the author can **commit with one click**.
- **Figma/Notion:** comment vs. assigned action item.

This maps directly onto the envelope's `intent` field (BD-D `ADDITIONS`), but I'd push harder than "a tag": **intent should change the envelope's downstream contract**, not just its color.
- `intent: change-request` → the member is expected to return a **proposed diff against the resolved `code://`/`board://` source**, and Tim gets an **Accept/Reject** surface (the brief's "make choices/selections if offered"). This is the Docs/GitHub suggestion loop, reframed: *the member proposes, mark-is-truth folds the acceptance, the source updates.* That closes Tim's "I want this specific thing updated" with a verifiable, reversible, one-tap action — which fits the company's git-revert-is-the-safety posture perfectly.
- `intent: question` / `idea` → a thread, no diff expected.
- `intent: bug` (UI mode) → carries the auto-screenshot + computed element state.

Without this split, *every* annotation becomes an ambiguous "do something with this," and the member has to guess whether Tim wants a discussion or an edit — re-introducing exactly the effort Tim said he wants removed.
