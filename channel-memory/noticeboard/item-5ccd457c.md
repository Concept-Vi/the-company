## 4. The PASS — review is not one stream of marks, it's structured rounds

No professional reviews once. We work in **passes**, and each pass has a *type*: a developmental pass (structure/argument), a line pass (sentence-level), a copy/proof pass (mechanics). A lawyer does a "first markup," sends it, gets the counterparty's, does a "second markup against their changes." **The pass is the unit of work, not the individual mark.**

The brief has the right atom — Tim's "make a dozen, submit them all" batch (BD-F) — but it's framed as a *delivery convenience* ("don't auto-send"). It's actually a **review pass**. Reframing it that way unlocks a lot:

- A batch/pass has a **focus** ("I'm reviewing the dragnet section tonight," "I'm doing a UI polish pass"). That focus is context the supervisor and members should *receive* — it tells them the lens to read all the marks through. A pass-level note is something I always write at the top of a returned manuscript: "Overall: argument is strong, but §3 needs evidence." That's a mark on the *whole pass*, not any one block.
- A pass is the natural **resolution boundary**. "Have all marks from my Tuesday pass been addressed?" is the question a reviewer actually asks. Per-mark status (received/working/replied/applied — BD-D ADDITIONS) rolls up into per-pass status.
- This maps cleanly onto design-map's "session concept for an annotation pass" (BD-K INTENT-LEVEL) — confirm it, and make the **pass a first-class node** the marks hang off, exactly as the run becomes the hinge-node for dragnet cards (A7 step 2). Same shape, reused.

**Steal this convention:** when Tim opens the overlay to submit, the submit screen *is* the pass summary — "12 marks: 4 changes, 5 queries, 2 notes, 1 approve" grouped by verb, each tappable to its anchor. He reviews his own pass before sending, the way an editor flips back through their marks before returning the manuscript. That's the professional submit ritual, and it's far more useful than a bare "send all" button.
