## I. THE TWO SPACES AND THE LAW OF PLACEMENT **[TIM]**

Every unit in the system has positions in **two coordinate systems at once**:

- **Structural space** — objective, discrete, exact: size, age, filetype, address/path, type. Each property is one-dimensional; a value is only meaningful relative to at least one other value plus an external context (the definition of a measurement). Addresses have a structural side AND a type side, and the type side is hierarchical like everything else.
- **Semantic space** — continuous, learned, relational: where the unit sits in meaning. There are as many semantic coordinates as there are lenses (one per embedder/instruction).

**The Convergence Law.** *Actual information is values where the structural and semantic converge — at the points where discrete and continuous are the same.* The optimal position of a unit is where its discrete description (where the system says it is) and its continuous description (what it actually means) coincide. Units can sit anywhere in either space, but the further from a shared point, the more loss/friction; the closer, the less. **Resistance = 0 at convergence.** The maximum-divergence points sit at odd multiples of π/4, at √2/2 — formally [GEN]: the deep holes of the integer lattice, the bin boundaries of a quantizer, the decision boundaries of a classifier (maximum-entropy points). A well-formed system places no valid unit at a deep hole.

Friction is not metaphor: it is repeated computational work caused by coordinate disagreement (the file whose name lies about its contents pays a reconciliation tax on every access, forever, until repaired).

**Frame-relativity.** Discrete-vs-continuous is not a property of reality but of the chosen frame: the axes are variables too, relative to the unit — **scale and state/phase are the axes that select the frame** (the same content is one unit at project-scale and a stream at exchange-scale; the same H₂O presents different coordinate systems as ice/water/vapor). A type declaration at a scale defines what the axes ARE for units at that scale: the registry holds coordinate systems, not just categories.

**Preferred systems** are those with the least deviation, that **consume and generate equally** — steady state, detailed balance, impedance matching [GEN names].

**The perfect form** [TIM]: every address occupies a convergence point, every point is occupied with reachable existence, no addresses at divergent points — then any movement is perfect, free, no effort; any integer division between extremities has the same shape and properties in every possible move. [GEN]: a homogeneous space with full symmetry — the perfect crystal at ground state; superconductivity is the physical proof that sufficient order makes resistance literally zero (Noether: symmetry ⇒ conservation ⇒ free movement).

**Real systems** [TIM]: real things are not perfect, so action takes energy and processing — the small models consume GPU exactly according to load, zero when idle. Real data is never perfect; no conversation can be a perfect transfer — *that is why resources exist*. [GEN]: Landauer — deviation costs energy, always. **The design doctrine that follows:** the perfect lattice is unreachable, but every unit moved closer to convergence (typed, addressed, evenly divided, axis-disciplined) permanently lowers the resistance of every future operation that touches it. **Laws are not constraints; laws are resistance-removal — paid once, dividending forever.** This is the thermodynamic ground under the path-of-least-resistance law.

**The Company** [TIM]: *"with the resources that I have and my rules applied — not conventional or generic — that model at its core is what the Company is."* The registry/address space = lattice sites; the typed directional operations = legal moves; the swarm = the load-proportional working medium; the laws = symmetry constraints; deviation = resistance = cost = error. Conventional architecture places things by convention, not at convergence — and pays the disambiguation tax for the system's lifetime. The Company is the refusal of that tax.

## II. THE CLASSIFICATION LAW **[TIM]**

*Any type used for tiny-model classification or routing must declare: ONE axis, TWO extremes, and evenly spaced possible classifications between them.*

- Any integer division is technically legal, but there is a fall-off; for a 0.8B-class model the working range is ~**3–5** (Tim's stated intuition; [GEN] formally: the model's per-call channel capacity, log₂(3)≈1.6 to log₂(5)≈2.3 bits — an empirically calibratable constant per model).
- Discriminations beyond capacity must be composed as **progressive chains**: 3 classifications from 3 options beats 1 from 9 (3×log₂3 bits moved reliably > log₂9 moved unreliably). The path can differ at each step — adaptive decision trees; the resolved context/content of each step feeds the next. Concurrent tiny models are extremely fast: chains are cheap, depth is nearly free (0.8B ran optimally ~120 concurrent).
- Structural measures (size, age, date — programmatic, free, exact) pair with the tiny-model semantic read — *measure structurally first because it is free; spend the model only on what structure cannot see.*
- Even spacing is the water law: distinctions, like water around a shared origin, settle to even distribution — [GEN] uniform quantization is minimax-optimal (minimizes the worst-case distance of any real value to its nearest valid position = keeps everything as far from the √2/2 deep hole as possible). **The Classification Law and the Convergence Law are one law, two faces.**
- Sibling laws in the same rulebook: **edges must be directional along an axis with a meaningful inverse** (precedes/succeeds, contains/is-contained-by); **registry-is-truth** (the types classified against ARE registry entries — one type declaration updates classification, decomposition, and routing everywhere). Everything legal in the system is axis-disciplined; free-floating labels and undirected relations are illegal because they have no coordinates.
