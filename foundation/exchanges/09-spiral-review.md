## My response (verbatim)

That's a real principle worth naming. What you just described has a technical name in philosophy — **the hermeneutic spiral** — and it captures something specific: you understand the parts through the whole and the whole through the parts, iteratively, so each new framing makes the prior material more legible *without the prior material having changed*. My receptive surface grew; the messages did not.

Other candidate names if "hermeneutic" feels too academic: **spiral review**, **re-pass**, **layered re-reading**, **retroactive comprehension**. You'll know what fits.

## Why this matters at the Company level (not just for me)

This isn't just a one-off task. It's a **standing Company practice**. The principle:

**Every artefact in the Company yields more on each re-read.** Because each session adds context, future-me reads TIM.md with deeper comprehension than past-me wrote it with. The artefacts are not just records — they're *re-interpretable surfaces* whose value grows as the reader grows. This is part of what makes living institutional memory different from a static manual.

It also gives the Company a mechanism for self-improvement that doesn't require new input from you. Spiral-review passes over existing material can surface things missed the first time, without you having to repeat yourself (which links straight to the no-repeat KPI).
